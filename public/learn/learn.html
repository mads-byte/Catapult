Lesson 1: Smart Spending Habits

Smart Spending Habits

Being mindful of how you spend your money can help you save more for what truly matters Some smart spending habits are:

  • Spending within your means: Setting budgets based on your income or allowance helps you understand what you can and can't afford
  • Avoid impulse purchases: Avoid those impulsive online shopping sprees. Instead, leave items in your shopping cart for a day or two before checking out. This gives you time to think through it and reflect if you realy need it
  • Reduce non-essential expenses: Take some time to reflect on how you spend your money. Make a list of your most common expenses. Then review each non-essential expense to see if you can cut back on anything. For example: Do you really need to go on a coffee run every morning?
  • Distinguish between needs and wants: Before you buy something ask yourself do you really need this item? or do you just want it for the moment and are going to forget about it later?

Lesson 2: Taxes

There are different kinds of taxes, but there's one that affects you every day: Sales Tax. It's when you pay a percentage of the cost of your purchase in addition to the actual cost of the item. You know when those jeans you really want to buy say they cost $36.99 on the tag, but they're a slightly higher price when you get to the register? That's because sales tax has been applied. The sales tax rate varies from state to state. Some states have high sales tax, while other states don't have any sales tax at all.
The next one is very important when you get a job: Income Tax. You are responsible for paying the government a portion of all the money you make. The more you earn, the more you owe. When you earn more, you move up to a higher tax bracket. Tax brackets categorize people based on their income in order to determine how much they need to pay. The government agency responsible for enforcing and collecting taxes is called the Internal Revenue Service, more commonly known as the IRS. The IRS investigates and fines those who do not pay. Underpaying or refusing to pay can even result in jail time.
If all of this sounds scary, don't worry. Employers are responsible for withholding the taxes from their employees' income and reporting how much was withheld. First, they give a new employee a form called a W-4. It is imperative that the employee fills out this form accurately because this will ensure that the correct amount is withheld and paid to the IRS. Then, the employee will receive a form annually called a W-2 which will tell the employee just how much their employer has withheld for taxes.

If you're thinking about starting a business or selling a product, it is best to seek out tax advice from a trained professional.
